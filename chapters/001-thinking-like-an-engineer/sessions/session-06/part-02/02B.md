# Session 06 — From One Computer to a Scalable System

## Part 2B — From One Powerful Computer to a Reliable System

---

# Reasoning Journey (Continued)

## Stage 1 — The Most Obvious Solution

Once it became clear that a personal laptop could no longer support a rapidly growing platform, the most obvious solution naturally came to mind.

> **"If one computer is insufficient, why not simply purchase a much more powerful one?"**

At first glance, this appears to be a perfectly logical solution.

A more powerful processor can execute more operations every second.

Additional memory allows more applications and requests to remain active simultaneously.

Larger storage devices can accommodate a much greater collection of videos.

From this perspective, growth seems to require only one thing:

**A bigger computer.**

This is often the first solution people imagine because it solves the most visible problems—speed and storage.

However, engineering rarely ends with the first reasonable answer.

Instead, every proposed solution should be challenged by asking:

> **"What new problems does this solution introduce?"**

---

## Stage 2 — The Investor's Thought Experiment

To examine this idea more carefully, a hypothetical scenario was considered.

Imagine an investor approaches the platform owner and says:

> *"Money is no longer a limitation. I will provide enough resources to purchase the most powerful computer available. It has enormous storage capacity, exceptional processing power and can easily serve millions of users."*

At first, this appears to solve every previously identified limitation.

The platform now has:

* Vast storage.
* Powerful processors.
* High-performance memory.
* Fast response times.

If performance were the only concern, the problem would appear solved.

But engineering requires looking beyond ordinary situations.

The discussion therefore shifted to a different question.

> **"What happens if this single computer suddenly fails?"**

---

## Stage 3 — Discovering the Single Point of Failure

Suppose the computer experiences one of the following situations:

* A hardware component fails.
* The power supply stops functioning.
* A storage device becomes corrupted.
* An unexpected technical fault occurs.
* Routine maintenance requires the machine to be shut down.

Although the computer was extremely powerful, every service provided by the platform immediately becomes unavailable.

Users cannot upload videos.

Existing videos cannot be watched.

Search no longer functions.

Comments cannot be loaded.

The entire platform becomes inaccessible because every responsibility depended upon a single machine.

This observation revealed an important weakness.

Increasing the capability of one component does not necessarily increase the reliability of the entire system.

The platform remained vulnerable because everything depended upon one point of failure.

---

## Stage 4 — A Different Way of Thinking

Rather than making one computer increasingly powerful, another possibility emerged.

Instead of asking:

> **"How can one computer perform more work?"**

The discussion gradually changed to:

> **"Why should one computer perform all the work in the first place?"**

This change in perspective represents one of the most significant moments in the session.

The objective was no longer to improve an individual machine.

The objective became designing a collection of machines that cooperate.

The proposed architecture evolved as follows.

```text
                 Users
                    │
                    ▼
      ┌─────────────────────────┐
      │     Platform System     │
      └─────────────────────────┘
                    │
        ┌───────────┼───────────┐
        ▼           ▼           ▼
   Computer A   Computer B   Computer C
```

Instead of assigning every task to a single computer, the workload could now be shared among several independent systems.

Each computer contributes to the overall operation of the platform.

Together, they behave as one larger system.

---

## Stage 5 — Why Many Computers Are Better Than One

Initially, the idea of multiple computers seemed attractive because it increased processing capacity.

However, upon closer examination, another advantage became apparent.

Suppose one of the computers unexpectedly stops functioning.

```text
Computer A   ✅

Computer B   ❌

Computer C   ✅
```

Unlike the previous architecture, the entire platform does not necessarily stop operating.

The remaining computers continue serving users while the faulty machine can be repaired or replaced.

This realization introduced an important engineering concept.

A well-designed system should continue functioning even when some of its individual components fail.

Reliability therefore becomes an architectural property rather than a hardware property.

---

## Stage 6 — A New Concern

Although the network of computers solved one important problem, it immediately raised another question.

If several computers now exist, what exactly should each one do?

Should every computer perform every task?

Should each computer store all uploaded videos?

Or should responsibilities be divided?

At this point, no complete answer was available.

However, the discussion revealed an important engineering habit.

Solving one problem often exposes a deeper one.

The purpose of engineering is therefore not to eliminate every question at once, but to move progressively toward a better understanding.

---

# Refined Understanding

The initial instinct was to solve the platform's growth by purchasing a more powerful computer.

This solution successfully addressed concerns related to storage and processing speed.

However, a deeper examination exposed a fundamental weakness.

The architecture still depended entirely upon a single machine.

No matter how powerful that machine became, its failure would still interrupt every service offered by the platform.

This realization shifted the focus away from hardware and toward architecture.

Instead of designing stronger computers, engineers design stronger systems.

A system composed of several cooperating computers can continue functioning even when individual machines fail.

Growth therefore requires more than increased computing power.

It requires distributing responsibilities across multiple participants so that no single component becomes indispensable.

---

# Engineering Principle

> **The strength of a system is not measured by the power of its strongest component.**

> **It is measured by its ability to continue functioning when individual components fail.**

This marks the beginning of a new way of thinking.

Engineering is no longer about individual machines.

It becomes the study of systems composed of many cooperating parts.

# Reflection

This stage represents a fundamental shift in engineering thinking.

At the beginning of the discussion, increasing hardware capability appeared to be the natural response to increasing demand.

By the end of this reasoning journey, the emphasis had shifted completely.

The objective was no longer to create the most powerful computer.

The objective became designing a system that remains available, reliable and adaptable even when individual components encounter unexpected failures.

The question that naturally follows is therefore:

> **If many computers work together, how should responsibilities and information be divided among them?**

The answer to this question forms the foundation of the next stage of the session.
