# Session 06 — From One Computer to a Scalable System

## Part 3A — Rethinking Distribution Through the Library Analogy

---

# Reasoning Journey (Continued)

## Stage 7 — A New Challenge Appears

By the end of the previous stage, the architecture had already evolved from a single powerful computer to a network of cooperating computers.

The platform had become more reliable because it no longer depended upon one machine.

However, an important question still remained unanswered.

> **If many computers now exist, how should information be distributed among them?**

This question appears simple, yet it immediately introduces several design possibilities.

Should every computer store every uploaded video?

Should each computer store only a portion of the available information?

Should there be one central location responsible for preserving everything?

At this point, no answer seemed completely satisfactory.

Rather than searching for existing technological solutions, the discussion once again returned to first principles.

Instead of beginning with computers, a real-world system was chosen whose purpose is remarkably similar:

**A library.**

---

## Stage 8 — Constructing the First Library Model

Imagine that a writer completes a new book.

Millions of readers around the world wish to read it.

The first responsibility belongs to the organisation that receives the original work.

Within the analogy, this became the **World Library**.

The World Library preserves the original copy and becomes responsible for making the work available to the rest of the world.

The first proposed architecture therefore appeared as follows.

```text
Author
   │
   ▼
World Library
   │
   ▼
National Libraries
   │
   ▼
Local Libraries
   │
   ▼
Readers
```

Each level possesses a different responsibility.

The World Library safeguards the original material.

National Libraries coordinate distribution throughout their respective countries.

Local Libraries interact directly with readers and make the book available within their communities.

At first glance, this architecture appeared organised, scalable and practical.

Information gradually flows from higher levels of the hierarchy towards readers.

---

## Stage 9 — Discovering an Unexpected Weakness

While describing this architecture, another observation naturally emerged.

The same book was being copied repeatedly.

The sequence appeared something like this.

1. The author submits one original copy.
2. The World Library creates copies for many National Libraries.
3. Each National Library creates additional copies for Local Libraries.
4. Local Libraries may continue producing further copies whenever necessary.

This immediately raised an important concern.

> **Are we processing the same information too many times?**

The intention behind the architecture was to improve accessibility.

However, the process itself now required repeated copying at every level.

The discussion therefore shifted from reliability towards efficiency.

The architecture solved one problem but appeared to introduce another.

---

## Stage 10 — Self-Critique

One of the most important moments of the discussion occurred here.

Instead of defending the proposed architecture, it was questioned.

A reflection emerged:

> **"I am not satisfied with my proposed solution."**

This statement represents an important engineering habit.

Good engineers do not become attached to their first design.

Instead, they actively search for its weaknesses.

Finding limitations early is far less expensive than discovering them after a system has already been built.

This moment therefore marked a transition from proposing ideas to critically evaluating them.

---

## Stage 11 — A More Refined Proposal

Rather than allowing every level of the hierarchy to continually produce copies, a new idea gradually formed.

The hierarchy itself would remain.

However, unnecessary duplication could be reduced.

The revised proposal suggested the following.

The World Library continues preserving the original material.

National Libraries receive copies only as part of the broader distribution process.

Local Libraries no longer produce unlimited copies automatically.

Instead, each Local Library maintains only a practical working stock.

Whenever readers request the book, additional copies can be produced as needed.

The refined architecture can be represented as follows.

```text
World Library
      │
      ▼
National Libraries
      │
      ▼
Local Libraries
      │
      ├── Small Ready Stock
      │
      ▼
Readers
```

Instead of distributing enormous numbers of unnecessary copies everywhere, resources are allocated more carefully.

Accessibility is preserved while unnecessary duplication is reduced.

---

## Stage 12 — From Copying Everything to Managing Demand

The discussion now reached a deeper insight.

The problem was no longer about making copies.

The real problem was deciding **when** copies should be made.

Two different philosophies now became apparent.

### Philosophy 1

Produce copies everywhere before anyone requests them.

Advantages:

* Immediate availability.

Disadvantages:

* Significant waste of storage, resources and effort.

---

### Philosophy 2

Maintain only a reasonable working stock.

Produce additional copies only when genuine demand appears.

Advantages:

* Better utilisation of resources.
* Reduced unnecessary duplication.
* Improved flexibility.

Disadvantages:

* Requires monitoring demand and responding efficiently.

Although neither approach is universally perfect, the second philosophy appeared considerably more balanced.

It recognises that engineering is rarely about choosing extremes.

Instead, good systems attempt to balance competing objectives.

---

# Refined Understanding

By this stage of the discussion, the understanding had evolved beyond simply distributing information across many locations.

Initially, the objective had been to ensure that information remained available everywhere.

However, deeper analysis revealed that unrestricted duplication introduces its own costs.

Every additional copy requires storage, processing and management.

Consequently, a scalable architecture should not focus solely on availability.

It must also consider the efficient use of resources.

The discussion therefore shifted towards a more mature perspective.

Instead of asking:

> **"Where should copies exist?"**

The more important question became:

> **"Under what circumstances should new copies be created?"**

This subtle change transformed the problem from one of storage into one of intelligent resource management.

---

# Engineering Principle

> **Making more copies is not always the best solution.**

> **The objective of engineering is not maximum duplication, but the appropriate balance between availability and efficiency.**

# Reflection

This stage demonstrates another important characteristic of engineering reasoning.

The first solution often appears convincing because it addresses the original problem.

However, once examined carefully, that same solution may introduce new inefficiencies.

Rather than abandoning the architecture entirely, the discussion refined it by questioning each assumption and preserving only those ideas that continued to provide value.

This process of continual refinement is one of the defining characteristics of engineering design.

The next stage of the discussion explores how demand itself can guide distribution and why prediction should assist decision-making without replacing real-world observation.
